NTIA Technical Report TR-08-453
Intelligibility of Selected Radio Systems in the Presence of Fireground Noise:
Test Plan and Results

Authors: David J. Atkinson, Andrew A. Catellier
Published: June 2008
Publisher: U.S. Department of Commerce, National Telecommunications and
  Information Administration (NTIA)
Conducted at: Institute for Telecommunication Sciences (ITS), Boulder, Colorado

OVERVIEW

This report documents the first systematic measurement of radio communication
intelligibility under realistic fireground noise conditions. The study was prompted
by widespread complaints from fire agencies (Boise, Fairfax, Littleton, and Phoenix
Fire departments) that P25 digital radio systems suffered severe intelligibility
problems during active fire operations. The International Association of Fire Chiefs
(IAFC) formed a Digital Problem Working Group (DPWG) to investigate, and ITS
developed this test plan in conjunction with the DPWG Testing Subcommittee.

The core question: how do P25 digital vocoders compare to legacy analog FM radios
when a firefighter is transmitting in a high-noise environment while wearing SCBA
(self-contained breathing apparatus)?

COMMUNICATION SYSTEMS TESTED

Four radio systems were evaluated, each using a cabled (ideal) RF path between
transmit and receive units to isolate vocoder performance from channel effects:

  System 1 -- 25 kHz Analog FM radio pair. The reference standard that fire
    agencies had used since the mid-20th century.
  System 2 -- P25 Full Rate (QFA): Baseline full-rate IMBE vocoder at 7200 bps
    gross bit rate (4400 b/s net + 2800 b/s FEC), based on the original 1992
    vocoder selection, with manufacturer speech processing enhancements.
  System 3 -- P25 Enhanced Full Rate (QFB): Enhanced full-rate IMBE vocoder at
    7200 bps, incorporating non-essential improvements developed since 1992 plus
    manufacturer enhancements.
  System 4 -- 12.5 kHz Analog FM radio pair. Tested in only three environments
    to evaluate narrowband compliance viability.

NOISE ENVIRONMENTS

Nine transmission environments were defined, chosen by a panel of fire
practitioners from the IAFC testing committee. The noise recordings were captured
as high-quality digital recordings at a fire agency training facility:

  Env 1: No background noise, no mask (Clean condition)
  Env 2: Fire truck pump panel, no mask (SNR 4 dB)
  Env 3: SCBA mask with no background noise
  Env 4: Two PASS alarms sounding, with mask (SNR -2 dB + ~9 dB mask loss)
  Env 5: In-mask low air alarm (SNR 15 dB + mask loss)
  Env 6: Rotary saw cutting metal garage door, with mask (SNR 4 dB + mask loss)
  Env 7: Chainsaw cutting wood, with mask (SNR 5 dB + mask loss)
  Env 8: 2.5-inch fire hose with fog nozzle, with mask (SNR 9 dB + mask loss)
  Env 9: Rotary saw cutting metal garage door, with amplified mask (SNR 4 dB)

Six of these noises were recorded specifically for this study: fire truck pump
panel, dual PASS alarms, low air alarm, fog nozzle, rotary saw, and chainsaw.
Power spectral density plots and spectrograms for each noise are provided in the
report. The SCBA mask introduces approximately 9 dB of acoustic path loss,
particularly in the 1500-3000 Hz range critical to speech intelligibility.

TEST METHODOLOGY -- MODIFIED RHYME TEST (MRT)

The study used the Modified Rhyme Test (MRT) per ANSI S3.2, which is also
required by NFPA 1981-2007 for SCBA evaluation. In an MRT, speakers read carrier
sentences ("Please select the word...") followed by a target word. Listeners
choose from six rhyming alternatives (e.g., bed, led, fed, red, wed, shed).

Speech material was recorded from 6 talkers (3 male, 3 female) in a quiet
environment. Clean speech was played through a Head and Torso Simulator (HATS)
at 100 dBC while noise was generated by loudspeakers in an NC-35 attenuated
chamber. The combined signal was transmitted through each radio system. On the
receive end, audio was captured through a second HATS in a quiet environment.

A panel of 30 listeners (fire service practitioners with audiometrically normal
hearing) participated in listening tests at ITS. Each listener scored 1500
samples across 5 sessions. Intelligibility scores were adjusted for guessing
probability (RA metric). Results were analyzed using one-way ANOVA (alpha=0.01)
with Tukey multiple comparison tests to determine statistical significance.

KEY FINDINGS

Environment 1 (Clean): All four systems exceeded the NFPA 80% intelligibility
  goal. Analog FM systems (25 kHz: 0.881, 12.5 kHz: 0.886) were statistically
  better than both digital systems (P25 Full Rate: 0.826, P25 Enhanced: 0.800).

Environment 2 (Pump Panel): All systems dropped dramatically. The 25 kHz Analog
  (0.437) and P25 Enhanced Full Rate (0.470) were statistically equivalent and
  both significantly better than P25 Full Rate (0.341).

Environment 3 (Mask Only): Analog systems maintained near-80% intelligibility
  (25 kHz: 0.785, 12.5 kHz: 0.798) while digital systems dropped significantly
  (P25 Full Rate: 0.522, P25 Enhanced: 0.591). The mask alone caused a major
  intelligibility penalty for the IMBE vocoders.

Environment 5 (PASS Alarms): The largest analog-vs-digital gap. Analog FM scored
  0.581 while P25 Full Rate scored only 0.152 and P25 Enhanced scored 0.206.
  The PASS alarm's tonal characteristics severely degraded vocoder performance.

Environments 4, 6, 7, 8 (Low Air, Rotary Saw, Chainsaw, Fog Nozzle): All systems
  scored below 10% intelligibility -- effectively unintelligible for all
  technologies. These environments were too difficult for any radio system.

Environment 9 (Amplified Mask): P25 Enhanced Full Rate (0.092) significantly
  outperformed P25 Full Rate (0.015), with Analog FM (0.039) in between.

SUMMARY OF TECHNOLOGY COMPARISONS

- Analog FM outperformed P25 Full Rate in 5 of 9 environments.
- Analog FM outperformed P25 Enhanced Full Rate in 4 of 9 environments.
- P25 Full Rate and Enhanced Full Rate were statistically equivalent in 8 of 9
  environments; Enhanced was better in 1 (Environment 9).
- 12.5 kHz Analog FM was statistically equivalent to 25 kHz Analog FM in all
  three environments where it was tested.
- The MRT intelligibility test was notably more discriminating than MOS tests,
  detecting statistically significant differences between P25 Full Rate and
  Enhanced Full Rate that MOS-based tests (BABE, BABF) had not found.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR TIA-102.BABG

Six of the fireground noise recordings from this study (fire truck pump panel,
dual PASS alarm, low air alarm, fog nozzle, rotary saw, and chainsaw) were
adopted by TIA-102.BABG as standard test noise environments for the enhanced
vocoder performance measurement suite. They are referenced as informative
reference [4.2.5] in BABG. These recordings, originally captured at 48 kHz
sample rate, provide the real-world acoustic conditions that any P25 vocoder
claiming "enhanced" performance must be tested against.

VOCODER PERFORMANCE IMPLICATIONS

The SCBA mask alone (Environment 3) caused a 26-33 percentage point drop in
intelligibility for the digital vocoders versus only 10 points for analog FM.
This demonstrates that the IMBE vocoder's parametric speech model is
fundamentally more sensitive to the acoustic distortion introduced by breathing
apparatus than waveform-based analog transmission. The mask's ~9 dB attenuation
in the 1500-3000 Hz band distorts the spectral envelope that the MBE model
relies on for voiced/unvoiced decisions and spectral amplitude estimation.

The PASS alarm results (Environment 5) are particularly relevant: the alarm's
strong tonal components appear to confuse the vocoder's pitch estimation,
causing near-total loss of intelligibility for the digital systems while analog
FM preserves enough speech content for moderate intelligibility (58%).

These findings directly motivated the P25 community to pursue enhanced vocoder
algorithms and establish objective performance thresholds (BABG) that account
for these challenging acoustic conditions.
