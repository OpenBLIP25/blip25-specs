WHAT THIS DOCUMENT SPECIFIES

TSB-88.3-C is Part 3 of a three-part bulletin series that provides recommended
methods for technology-independent performance verification of land mobile radio
(LMR) systems operating below 1 GHz. While Parts 1 and 2 cover performance
modeling and propagation modeling respectively, Part 3 focuses on empirical
validation -- the methods used to confirm that a deployed system actually achieves
its predicted coverage and performance in the presence of noise and interference.

The document addresses three core areas: Coverage Acceptance Testing (CATP) for
both voice and data systems, Local Conformance Measurements for interference
dispute resolution, and Interference Effects analysis including intermodulation
modeling and identification techniques.

TSB-88 SERIES CONTEXT

The TSB-88-C series was developed in response to requests from APCO International,
the Land Mobile Communications Council (LMCC), and the National Coordination
Committee (NCC, later NPSTC). The series addresses the increasing complexity of
spectrum management as wireless systems migrate to narrowband and bandwidth-
efficient technologies under FCC Spectrum Refarming mandates. It covers channel
spacings from 6.25 kHz to 30 kHz and frequency bands including VHF, UHF, 700 MHz,
800 MHz, and 900 MHz.

The three parts are:
  - TSB-88.1-C: Technology Independent Performance Modeling
  - TSB-88.2-C: Propagation Modeling, including Noise
  - TSB-88.3-C: Performance Verification (this document)

KEY METRICS AND THRESHOLDS

Channel Performance Criterion (CPC): The central metric is expressed as either
Cf/N (faded carrier to noise ratio) or Cf/(I+N) (faded carrier to interference
plus noise ratio) at a specified vehicular Doppler rate for a given DAQ level.
Systems are classified as either "Noise Limited" (dominated by the noise component)
or "Interference Limited" (dominated by the interference component).

DAQ (Delivered Audio Quality): Referenced throughout as the quality target for
voice systems. For public safety, DAQ 3.4 is recommended; for non-public-safety,
DAQ 3.0. DAQ is defined as a reference similar to Circuit Merit with additional
definitions for digitized voice and a static SINAD equivalent intelligibility
when subjected to multipath fading.

Coverage Reliability Targets: The document recommends 97% service area reliability
for public safety systems and 90% for commercial systems. A 99% confidence level
is recommended for conformance testing. The "estimate of proportions" formula
determines the minimum number of test tiles needed -- for example, 790 tiles for
a 99% confidence, 2% sampling error, 95% reliability target.

Standard BER: 5% is the standard bit error rate threshold per TIA-102.CAAA.

Reference Sensitivity: 12 dB SINAD is the standard reference for analog systems.
For P-25 digital (C4FM), the reference sensitivity with a Cs/N of 5 dB produces
a 5% BER at the standard reference level.

VOICE AND DATA COVERAGE ACCEPTANCE TESTING

Voice CATP (V-CATP): Tests are performed using either carrier power (RSSI) or BER
measurements. The service area is divided into uniform test tiles (recommended size
100-lambda by 100-lambda, not exceeding 2 km x 2 km). At each tile, a test sample
of at least 50 subsamples is collected over a distance of 40 wavelengths. The
document provides a comprehensive VCATP Test Matrix covering digital and analog
systems in single-site, simulcast, and multi-site configurations for both talk-out
and talk-in directions.

Data CATP (D-CATP): Two criteria are defined -- Message Success Rate (MSR) for
narrowband systems (<25 kHz) and Data Throughput Rate (DTR) for wideband and
broadband systems. MSR testing confirms message delivery in 90-95% of the service
area. DTR testing measures application throughput using UDP packets at multiple
speed ranges.

INTERFERENCE EFFECTS AND INTERMODULATION

The document provides extensive treatment of receiver intermodulation (IM) effects,
particularly relevant to sites with receiver multicouplers and tower-top amplifiers.
Key concepts include:

- Cascaded Noise Figure calculation using Friis' equation for multicoupler chains
- Third Order Intercept Point (IP3) determination and its relationship to IMR
- The Symbolic Method for computing absolute IM levels through gain chains
- Non-coherent power addition methods for combining noise and interference sources
- Noise-Adjusted Faded Performance Threshold (FPT_Adj) accounting for environmental
  noise above kT0b

NARROWBAND VS. WIDEBAND INTERMODULATION

A significant finding is that wideband data systems (e.g., SAM 50 kHz) exhibit
approximately 12.8 dB lower measured IMR than P-25 narrowband systems with the
same IP3, due to the wider receiver ENBW (42.6 kHz vs. 5.5 kHz) and higher C/N
requirements (16 dB vs. 7.6 dB). This has direct implications for frequency
coordination near wideband data sites.

INTERFERENCE IDENTIFICATION

The document provides a practical three-test methodology for local median
determination in interference disputes (originally developed for the 800 MHz
rebanding process). The tests progressively isolate the desired signal (C),
interference plus noise (I+N), and noise (N) components, with specific criteria
for public safety and commercial systems.

BUILDING PENETRATION

Generalized medium building penetration loss follows the formula:
  a = 36.6 - 7.6404 * log10(F_MHz)
with a slope of approximately -2.3 dB/octave. The document provides detailed
methods for measuring exterior wall loss and estimating in-building coverage,
noting that building loss generally decreases with height and varies significantly
with construction materials.

PRACTICAL SIGNIFICANCE

TSB-88.3-C bridges the gap between theoretical coverage prediction and real-world
system performance. For P25 system designers and frequency coordinators, it
provides the standardized measurement methodology needed for acceptance testing,
interference resolution, and spectrum management. Its technology-neutral approach
allows direct comparison between analog FM, P25 Phase 1 (C4FM/CQPSK), and
wideband data systems operating in shared or adjacent spectrum.
