NTIA Technical Report TR-01-386
Voice Quality Assessment of Vocoders in Tandem Configuration
Authors: Christopher Redding, Nicholas DeMinco, Jeanne Lindner
Institute for Telecommunication Sciences (ITS), Boulder, Colorado
Published: April 2001
U.S. Department of Commerce / National Telecommunications and Information Administration

OVERVIEW

This report documents objective voice quality testing of six vocoders operated
both individually and in pairwise tandem configurations. Tandem vocoding occurs
when speech is decoded from one vocoder back to analog (or PCM), then
immediately re-encoded by a second vocoder -- the scenario that arises whenever
two different digital radio systems are bridged together. The study was
performed by ITS for the National Communications System's Office of Standards
and Technology under reimbursable order DNRO 66008.

The central question is straightforward: how much does voice quality degrade
when vocoders are cascaded? The answer has direct consequences for P25 system
design, where ISSI bridging, console patching, and Phase 1 to Phase 2
conversion all create tandem vocoding opportunities.

VOCODERS TESTED

Six vocoders representing the major digital radio systems of the era were
evaluated:

  CELP   - FS-1016, 4.8 kbps, military, DSP board (single vocoder only)
  VSELP  - TIA/EIA/IS-54 TDMA, 8.0 kbps, DSP board
  QCELP  - TIA/EIA/IS-95 CDMA, 9.6 kbps variable rate, computer expansion board
  IMBE   - Project 25 (APCO), 7.2 kbps (4.4 speech + 2.8 FEC), DSP board
  AMBE   - Satellite systems, 4.8 kbps, DSP board
  ACELP  - TETRA, 7.2 kbps, software implementation

A 64 kbps PCM waveform coder (G.711) and a straight bypass (no coding) were
included as reference benchmarks. The CELP vocoder was tested only as a single
vocoder, not in tandem pairs. All other vocoders were tested in every pairwise
combination, with order reversed (A-B and B-A), yielding a comprehensive matrix
of tandem configurations.

TANDEM CONFIGURATIONS

Each tandem test connected two vocoders end-to-end: the analog output of
Vocoder A fed directly into the analog input of Vocoder B (decode then
re-encode through PCM). This is exactly the signal path that occurs in a
real-world bridge or gateway. Each pair was tested in both orderings. PCM
tandems (vocoder-PCM and PCM-vocoder) were also measured to isolate the effect
of adding a waveform coding stage. In total, the study covered 7 single
vocoders, 1 bypass reference, and approximately 30 tandem pair configurations.

MEASUREMENT METHODOLOGY

Voice quality was measured using the Audio Play, Record, and Estimate (APRE)
objective testing tool developed at ITS. APRE uses the Measuring Normalizing
Block (MNB) algorithm standardized in ITU-T Recommendation P.861. The algorithm
computes an Auditory Distance (AD) between the input and output speech signals
by analyzing spectral differences across multiple time and frequency scales,
emulating human perceptual adaptation. AD is transformed via a logistic function
into L(AD), which ranges from 0 (severe distortion) to 1 (transparent). L(AD)
is then mapped to an estimated Mean Opinion Score (MOS) using the formula
MOS = 4*L(AD) + 1, placing results on the standard 1-5 MOS scale.

Test material consisted of 40 Harvard Sentences (IEEE Standard Publication
No. 297) spoken by two male and two female English-language speakers. All tests
used simulated error-free channels, representing best-case performance.

KEY FINDINGS

Single vocoder estimated MOS scores:
  Bypass (no coding):  4.60
  PCM (64 kbps):       4.32
  QCELP:               3.46
  VSELP:               3.35
  IMBE:                3.32
  AMBE:                3.30
  ACELP:               3.28
  CELP:                3.03

All single vocoders scored between 3.0 and 3.5 -- communications grade quality.
The IMBE vocoder used by P25 Phase 1 scored 3.32, consistent with "Fair" on the
MOS scale.

Tandem vocoder-vocoder estimated MOS scores (selected):
  IMBE-IMBE:           2.59
  IMBE-AMBE:           2.63
  IMBE-VSELP:          2.75
  IMBE-ACELP:          3.01
  VSELP-QCELP:         2.54

Tandem vocoder-PCM estimated MOS scores (selected):
  IMBE-PCM:            3.19
  PCM-IMBE:            3.19
  VSELP-PCM:           3.37

Critical degradation pattern: Single vocoders score in the 3.0-3.5 range. In
vocoder-vocoder tandem, MOS drops to 2.5-3.0 -- a loss of approximately 0.5 to
0.8 MOS points. This moves quality from "Fair" toward "Poor." For PCM tandems,
the degradation is smaller (scores between 3.0 and 3.6), since PCM is nearly
transparent. The MOS decrease of approximately 0.5 points for tandem
configurations represents a perceptually significant degradation -- a decrease
in voice quality that is noticeable and objectionable to the untrained listener.

An unexpected result was that VSELP-PCM tandems actually showed slightly higher
L(AD) values than VSELP alone, likely because the PCM bandpass filtering
produced a signal slightly easier for the VSELP vocoder to encode. This may
also reflect a limitation of the objective estimator.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR P25 SYSTEMS

This study provides the empirical foundation for a critical P25 design
principle: avoid tandem vocoding wherever possible. Every time a P25 voice call
passes through a bridge, gateway, console patch, or cross-system link that
decodes to PCM and re-encodes, quality drops by roughly half a MOS point. Chain
two such transitions and the signal approaches unusable quality.

This is precisely why TIA-102.BABA-A Section 17 specifies parametric rate
conversion for Phase 1 (IMBE, FDMA) to Phase 2 (AMBE+2, TDMA) bridging.
Rather than decoding IMBE to PCM and re-encoding with AMBE+2 (tandem
vocoding), parametric conversion transfers the vocoder parameters directly --
pitch, voiced/unvoiced decisions, spectral magnitudes -- from one codec's
parameter space to the other's. This avoids the decode-to-analog-re-encode
chain entirely, preserving quality at or near single-vocoder levels.

The same principle applies to ISSI (Inter-RF Subsystem Interface) design per
the TIA-102.BACA series: when two P25 RFSS nodes bridge calls, passing IMBE
parameters directly across the ISSI link avoids tandem vocoding. Console
patches and cross-system gateways that decode to analog and re-encode represent
the worst case documented in this report.

The report's tests were conducted under ideal error-free channel conditions.
Real-world performance with channel errors, noise, and multipath would show
even greater degradation in tandem configurations, making the avoidance of
unnecessary vocoder cascading even more important in operational P25 systems.
